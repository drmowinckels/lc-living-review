# Consultation Response: Utmattelse, langvarig utmattelse, inkl. ME/CFS

Helsedirektoratet's consultation draft, published 4 February 2026.
Deadline: 4 May 2026.

---

## Field 1: Does the guideline meet its stated purpose?

Athanasia Monika Mowinckel

No, the guideline does not meet its stated purpose of ensuring evidence-based and responsible healthcare for patients with ME/CFS. The main problem is that the guideline explicitly does not distinguish between recommendations for patients with prolonged fatigue and patients with ME/CFS. ME/CFS is classified as a neurological disease (ICD-10 G93.3, ICD-11 8E49) with post-exertional malaise (PEM) as its cardinal symptom. Prolonged fatigue is a non-specific symptom with many different causes. These patient groups have different pathophysiology and respond differently to treatment.

By combining them, the guideline applies evidence from broad "chronic fatigue" populations directly to ME/CFS patients. Casson et al. (2023), which the guideline relies upon, includes 14 studies with 2,094 patients with broadly defined chronic fatigue — many without PEM. It is professionally irresponsible to use this evidence to recommend graded activity for ME/CFS patients without considering PEM status.

The guideline references NICE 2021 (NG206) but does not follow its central conclusions. NICE conducted the most rigorous evidence review of ME/CFS treatments ever undertaken and concluded that graded exercise therapy (GET) should not be offered and that activity management/pacing should be the primary approach. The Norwegian guideline reverses this conclusion by presenting "graded activity" as more effective than pacing — without discussing that this is effectively GET under a different name.

As a post-COVID patient and primary caretaker for my wife with ME/CFS, I know this from both sides. In January 2024, I became bedbound with fatigue, brain fog, and bodily pain. Showers sent my heart rate to 140 bpm and took three days to recover from. From February to June there was minimal improvement. The turning point came only when we found research on intensive pacing with 30-second breaks — not graded activity, but the opposite: doing less to get better. After eight weeks at Godthaab Rehabilitation Centre, with structured pacing as the core principle, I saw real progress. But every time I exceeded my capacity — a trip to the post office, a vet visit — it cost weeks of hard-won progress. This is not deconditioning. It is a physiological response I can measure in my heart rate data.

## Field 2: Missing, unclear, or irrelevant information

The guideline is missing critical information in several areas:

**1. Biomedical evidence is absent.** The guideline contains no references to biomedical ME/CFS research. Published research documents: immune dysfunction including T-cell dysfunction and NK cell failure (Peluso & Deeks, 2024); neuroinflammation demonstrated by PET imaging (Nakatomi et al., 2014); metabolic disturbances in energy metabolism (Germain et al., 2022); autonomic dysfunction with 71% prevalence of orthostatic intolerance (2025); microclots and endothelial dysfunction (Thierry, 2025); skeletal muscle pathology where exercise triggers worsening of mitochondrial dysfunction and immune cell infiltration (Appelman et al., 2024; Charlton et al., 2025). Norwegian researchers at Haukeland have published on endothelial dysfunction in ME/CFS (Sandvik et al., 2023). The NIH deep phenotyping study identified brain, immune, and metabolic abnormalities (Walitt et al., 2024). This research is entirely absent.

**2. Harm data is completely missing.** The guideline contains no discussion of adverse effects or harm from recommended interventions. Patient surveys consistently show 50–74% of ME/CFS patients report worsening from GET (Geraghty et al., 2019). NICE 2021 explicitly warned about harm from graded exercise. The Cochrane review on exercise therapy for CFS (Larun et al., 2019) is subject to formal complaints and its planned update has been discontinued (December 2024). Absence of safety data is a serious omission.

**3. PEM is undervalued.** The guideline recognises PEM diagnostically via the Canada Criteria but treats it as partly cognitive/attentional in treatment recommendations. Two-day CPET studies demonstrate objective, measurable reductions in VO2 and work capacity (Stussman et al., 2025). Muscle biopsies show exercise worsens mitochondrial dysfunction and triggers immune cell infiltration (Appelman et al., 2024). PEM is a physiological response, not a cognitive problem.

**4. Recovery narratives presented without methodological context.** Bakken et al. (2023) is a narrative study with severe selection bias and survivorship bias. Unreferenced patient testimony about "belief in recovery" being essential is included as professional rationale.

## Field 3: Suggestions for language improvements

**1. "Aktivitetsregulering" obscures important differences.** The umbrella term "activity regulation" masks the critical difference between pacing (stabilising, symptom-contingent) and graded activity (progressively increasing). These have different theoretical foundations and different risk profiles for patients with PEM. The guideline should use precise terms and clearly distinguish between approaches.

**2. "Langvarig utmattelse, inkl. ME/CFS" trivialises the diagnosis.** The title places ME/CFS as a subset of a non-specific symptom, contradicting its classification as a neurological disease (ICD-10 G93.3). Suggestion: "ME/CFS og langvarig utmattelse" or separate chapters with clearly differentiated recommendations.

**3. "Focus less on PEM" is medically misleading.** The phrase "cognitive techniques can help focus less on PEM and other symptoms" implies PEM is an attentional problem. PEM is an objective physiological phenomenon documented through muscle biopsies and CPET testing. This phrasing should be replaced with evidence-based guidance on PEM management through activity limitation and pacing.

**4. "Belief in recovery" as professional rationale.** The unreferenced claim that belief in recovery is "absolutely essential" for recovering from ME/CFS should be removed from the professional rationale. This implicitly places blame on patients who remain ill and is incompatible with biomedical understanding.

**5. "Bør" (should) vs. evidence quality.** The guideline uses "bør" (strong normative recommendation) for interventions with LOW to VERY LOW GRADE confidence. GRADE methodology indicates conditional/weak recommendations for low-quality evidence.

## Field 4: General comments on the draft

As a post-COVID patient and caretaker for my wife with ME/CFS, this consultation draft is deeply concerning. I have been on sick leave since January 2024. My breakthrough in recovery came not from graded activity or cognitive techniques — it came from accepting my physiological limitations and practising strict pacing. The guideline recommends the opposite of what has helped me, and risks causing harm to patients in a vulnerable situation.

The guideline presents a one-sided evidence base drawn exclusively from the CBT/GET research tradition while ignoring the substantial biomedical literature. This does not reflect current scientific knowledge.

**International context:** NICE 2021 (UK) withdrew GET and recommended energy management. The American Heart Association (2025) acknowledged that conventional exercise prescription does not apply to patients with PEM (Cornwell et al., 2025). Gloeckl et al. (2024) published evidence-based stratification of exercise recommendations by PEM status. The Norwegian guideline moves in the opposite direction from international consensus.

**Working group composition:** Four members filed formal dissents. ME-foreningen, ME-foreldrene, and Norsk Covidforening also submitted dissenting statements. Recovery Norge is represented on the working group — an organisation that promotes recovery through psychological approaches. The content of the dissents is not addressed in the guideline text.

**GRADE method vs. recommendation strength:** The guideline's own GRADE assessments show LOW to VERY LOW confidence in effect estimates. Yet "bør" (should) is used — a strong normative recommendation. This mismatch undermines the guideline's credibility.

**Consequences of implementation:** The guideline risks ME/CFS patients being prescribed graded activity that worsens their condition, PEM being dismissed as a cognitive problem, and Norway falling further behind international standards for ME/CFS care. Vink & Vink-Niese (2025) document that CBT and GET studies themselves demonstrate that ME/CFS is a physical disease.

## Field 5: Suggestions for implementation measures

**1. Separate ME/CFS from general fatigue.** Create separate recommendation chapters for patients with ME/CFS (diagnosed by Canada Criteria, with mandatory PEM) and patients with prolonged fatigue without ME/CFS. PEM fundamentally changes the risk-benefit assessment for activity-based interventions.

**2. Adopt PEM-stratified exercise recommendations.** Follow Gloeckl et al. (2024): no PEM — standard rehabilitation with monitoring; mild PEM — symptom-titrated, low-intensity activity below symptom threshold; moderate-severe PEM — pacing and energy management as primary intervention.

**3. Implement energy management/pacing as primary approach for PEM.** The Energy Envelope Theory has empirical support (Jason et al., 2013; O'Connor et al., 2019; Brown et al., 2013). The PACELOC trial showed personalised pacing reduced PEM (Godfrey et al., 2024). The PaceMe app showed improved PEM management (Meach et al., 2024).

**4. Include biomedical evidence.** The guideline must reference research on immune dysfunction, neuroinflammation, metabolic dysfunction, skeletal muscle pathology, microclots, and autonomic dysfunction. Include Norwegian research (Sandvik et al., 2023; Fluge/Mella group).

**5. Require PEM screening during assessment.** Ensure all patients with prolonged fatigue are systematically screened for PEM before treatment plans are developed. Patients with PEM should not be offered graded activity.

**6. Report harm data.** Any guideline recommending activity-based interventions for ME/CFS must report evidence of harm, including patient-reported outcomes.

**7. Training for healthcare professionals.** Implement mandatory training on PEM, pacing, and energy management for GPs and rehabilitation staff. Focus on recognising and respecting PEM as a physiological limitation.
